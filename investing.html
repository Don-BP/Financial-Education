The Most Powerful Force in Finance: Compound Interest (複利)

Why Isn't This Common Knowledge in Japan?

Warren Buffett said, "My wealth has come from a combination of living in America, some lucky genes, and compound interest." It is the engine of wealth creation, yet it's a concept rarely taught in Japanese schools. This gap in financial education leaves many people stuck in a cycle of saving without growing.

  • Simple Interest: You earn interest only on your initial investment (元本 - ganpon). It's linear, slow, and how a basic bank account works.
  • Compound Interest: You earn interest on your initial investment AND on the accumulated interest. It's "interest on interest," leading to exponential growth. It makes your money work for you.

An Illustration: Three Coffee Shops

☕ Shop A: The Hard Worker

The owner works every day, serves coffee, and earns a daily profit. If the owner stops working, the profit stops. This is like only earning a salary.

☕ Shop B: The Saver

The owner saves some profit in a cash box. The money is safe, but over time, its value decreases as the cost of beans and milk goes up (inflation).

☕ Shop C: The Investor

The owner uses profits to buy a better espresso machine. This machine makes more coffee faster, increasing profits, which are then used to open a second shop (compound growth).

The goal of smart financial planning is to build a "Shop C" system for your money.

The Cost of Doing Nothing: Financial Inertia

The biggest enemy to building wealth is often not making a "bad" decision, but making no decision at all. This inertia is fueled by two cultural traps:

  • The "Gaman" Trap: The spirit of endurance is admirable, but in finance, just 'enduring' a bad situation (like a 0.001% interest rate) means passively accepting a poorer future.
  • The "Boiling Frog" Syndrome: Financial erosion from inflation is slow and often unnoticed, like a frog in slowly heating water. It doesn't feel dangerous day-to-day, but the long-term impact is severe.
