A Foreigner's Guide to Finance in Japan

Navigating a new financial system can be confusing. Here are the answers to the most common questions we hear from foreign residents.

What Happens to My Pension if I Leave Japan?

This is the most critical question. If you are not a Japanese citizen and have paid into the pension system for at least 6 months, you are eligible for the Lump-sum Withdrawal Payment (脱退一時金 - Dattai Ichijikin) upon leaving Japan.

  • How it works: You apply within two years of permanently leaving Japan. You receive a refund based on your contribution period.
  • The Limitation: The refund calculation is currently capped at **5 years (60 months)** of payments, even if you paid for 10, 15, or more years. This means a significant portion of your contributions may not be returned.
  • The Consequence: Taking the lump-sum withdrawal voids all your contribution years. If you return to Japan later, your pension history starts from zero.

The Wake-Up Call: For long-term residents, the Lump-sum Withdrawal is often a poor financial choice. Understanding your options before you leave is critical.

NISA & iDeCo for Foreigners

As a resident of Japan (regardless of nationality), you can open and use both NISA and iDeCo accounts to grow your wealth tax-efficiently.

The Catch: Generally, if you cease to be a resident of Japan, you must close these accounts. This makes long-term planning difficult for those who are unsure about their future in Japan. It's crucial to have a strategy that accounts for this potential eventuality.

Remittance & Currency Risk

If your goal is to eventually move your savings back to your home country, you face currency risk. If the Yen is weak when you need to send money, your savings will be worth less in your home currency.

A sound financial strategy for an expat often involves diversifying not just in assets, but also in currencies, which can be achieved through specific global investment platforms accessible from Japan.
