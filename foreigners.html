A Foreigner's Guide to Finance in Japan

Navigating a new financial system can be confusing. Here are the answers to the most common questions we hear from foreign residents.

What Happens to My Pension if I Leave Japan?

This is the most critical question. If you are not a Japanese citizen and have paid into the pension system for at least 6 months, you are eligible for the Lump-sum Withdrawal Payment (脱退一時金 - Dattai Ichijikin) upon leaving Japan.

  • How it works: You apply within two years of permanently leaving Japan. You receive a refund based on your contribution period.
  • The Limitation: The refund calculation is currently capped at **5 years (60 months)** of payments, even if you paid for 10, 15, or more years. This means a significant portion of your contributions may not be returned.
  • The Consequence: Taking the lump-sum withdrawal voids all your contribution years. If you return to Japan later, your pension history starts from zero.

For Residents of 10+ Years: If you have contributed for 10 or more years, you are "vested" in the Japanese pension system and can no longer take the lump-sum. Instead, you are entitled to receive a Japanese pension for life, paid overseas, once you reach retirement age.

Social Security Agreements: Your Strongest Tool

What many expats don't know is that Japan has Social Security Totalization Agreements with around 20 countries, including the USA, UK, Canada, Germany, Australia, and more.

These agreements allow you to combine your periods of payment in both countries to meet the minimum eligibility requirements (usually 10 years) for a pension in both. For example, 7 years in Japan and 3 years in the US can allow you to meet the 10-year requirement for both. This prevents your paid premiums from being wasted.

Portability of Assets: Planning Beyond Borders

Due to currency risk and the limitations of domestic schemes if you leave Japan, a wise strategy for expats is to build an investment plan that is globally accessible and can be held in multiple currencies.
